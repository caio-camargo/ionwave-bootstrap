## Pitch Deck Purpose and Audience

### Primary Purpose (Confidence: A)

A pitch deck serves three core functions:

1. **Filter:** Separate interested investors from uninterested (speed to "no" is good)
2. **Educate:** Teach investor about market, problem, solution, business model
3. **Sell:** Persuade investor that this opportunity is worth their capital and time

**Key Insight:** A deck is not the investment decision — it's the gate to deeper conversations. Goal is to earn a meeting, not close the deal on slide 15.

### Target Audience

**Angels ($5-100K checks):**
- Review time: 2-5 minutes (skim mode)
- Decision criteria: Gut + founder + market size
- What they want: Clear problem/solution, credible team, reasonable ask
- Objections: "Market too small," "Why you?", "What's the exit?"

**Micro-VCs ($100-500K checks):**
- Review time: 5-10 minutes (analytical mode)
- Decision criteria: Traction + unit economics + TAM
- What they want: Evidence of demand, scalable model, venture-scale returns (10x+)
- Objections: "Show me traction," "Unit economics don't work," "Exit unclear"

**Family Offices ($50-500K checks):**
- Review time: 10-20 minutes (thorough mode)
- Decision criteria: Mission fit + downside protection + relationship
- What they want: Aligned values, capital preservation, patient growth
- Objections: "Too risky," "Timeline too long," "No strategic fit"

### Deck Journey (Confidence: A)

**Stage 1: Cold Email Attachment**
- Investor sees deck for first time (no context)
- Decides in 2-3 minutes: Meeting or pass?
- Must be self-explanatory (no narration)

**Stage 2: Pre-Meeting Review**
- Investor re-reads deck before scheduled call
- Prepares questions, identifies concerns
- Deck must anticipate objections

**Stage 3: Live Presentation**
- Founder presents deck in meeting (Zoom or in-person)
- Deck is visual aid, not script
- Must support conversation, not dominate it

**Stage 4: Forward to Partners**
- Investor shares deck with co-investors, advisors, partners
- Deck must work without founder present
- Clarity and credibility critical

## Slide-by-Slide Framework

### Standard Seed Deck Structure (12-15 slides)

**1. Cover Slide**
- Company name, tagline, logo
- Contact info (founder email, website)
- Confidentiality notice (optional)

**2. Problem Slide**
- What pain exists in the world?
- Who experiences this pain?
- How big is the pain? (frequency, intensity, willingness to pay)

**3. Solution Slide**
- How does your product solve the problem?
- Why is your approach better/different?
- Key features or benefits (3-5 bullets)

**4. Product Slide**
- What is the actual product? (photos, screenshots, demo)
- How does it work? (use case, user flow)
- Proof it exists (not vaporware)

**5. Market Slide**
- TAM (Total Addressable Market): How big is the universe?
- SAM (Serviceable Addressable Market): How much can you realistically reach?
- SOM (Serviceable Obtainable Market): What can you capture in 3-5 years?

**6. Traction Slide** (if applicable)
- Revenue, users, growth rate, retention
- Key metrics: CAC, LTV, MRR, churn
- Milestones achieved (product launch, first customers, partnerships)

**7. Business Model Slide**
- How do you make money? (pricing, revenue streams)
- Unit economics (COGS, margin, CAC, LTV)
- Path to profitability

**8. Competition Slide**
- Who else solves this problem?
- How are you different/better? (positioning map, comparison table)
- Unfair advantage or moat

**9. Go-to-Market Slide**
- How will you acquire customers? (channels, strategies)
- Marketing plan (content, ads, partnerships, influencers)
- Sales process (if B2B)

**10. Team Slide**
- Founders: Photos, names, titles, relevant experience
- Advisors or key hires (if impressive)
- Why is this team uniquely suited to win?

**11. Financials Slide**
- 3-5 year revenue projections
- Key assumptions (unit sales, pricing, growth rate)
- Path to profitability timeline

**12. Ask Slide**
- How much are you raising? (e.g., $500K SAFE)
- Valuation or cap (e.g., $3M cap)
- Use of funds (% breakdown: product, marketing, team, ops)

**13. Exit Slide** (optional but recommended)
- What are plausible exit scenarios? (acquisition, PE, IPO)
- Comparable exits (company X acquired for $Y)
- Timeline (3-5 years typical)

**14. Milestones Slide** (optional)
- What will you accomplish with this raise?
- 6-12 month roadmap (product, revenue, hiring)

**15. Closing Slide**
- Thank you + contact info
- Call to action: "Let's schedule a follow-up call"

## Visual Design Principles

### Core Principles (Confidence: A)

**1. Clarity > Creativity**
- Investors read 50+ decks per week — don't make them work hard
- Rule: If a slide requires >30 seconds to understand, simplify it
- Test: Show slide to friend for 10 seconds, ask what they learned

**2. Data Visualization**
- Use charts/graphs for financial projections, market size, traction
- Avoid: Tables with 20 rows (hard to parse), 3D pie charts (tacky), rainbow color schemes
- Prefer: Clean bar charts, line graphs, simple 2x2 matrices

**3. Visual Hierarchy**
- Most important info: Largest, bold, top-left (eye tracking starts there)
- Supporting info: Smaller, regular weight, below/right
- Use whitespace: Don't cram every pixel — breathing room improves comprehension

**4. Consistent Style**
- Pick 2-3 colors (brand colors + neutral) and stick to them
- Pick 1-2 fonts (headings + body) — avoid font chaos
- Template all slides (same header/footer, same spacing, same icon style)

**5. Investor-Friendly Palette**
- Avoid: Neon colors (harsh), pastels (looks amateurish), black backgrounds (hard to print)
- Prefer: Navy, gray, white (professional), brand accent color (1-2 slides for pop)

**6. Icon Usage**
- Use icons to break up text (problem = warning icon, solution = lightbulb, etc.)
- Source: Noun Project, Flaticon, Font Awesome (free, consistent style)
- Don't overdo it: 1-3 icons per slide max (more = clutter)

**7. One Idea Per Slide**
- Each slide should answer one question: "What's the problem?" "What's the solution?" "What's the market size?"
- If slide tries to answer 3 questions, split into 3 slides
- Exception: Comparison slides (competition table, positioning map) can have multiple data points

## Presentation Tips

### Pre-Presentation Preparation (Confidence: A)

**1. Know Your Metrics Cold**
- Investors will ask: "What's your CAC?" "What's your margin?" "How many units sold?"
- Practice until you can answer without looking at deck
- Have backup data ready: "CAC is $38 right now, was $45 last week, targeting $30-35 by Month 3"

**2. Anticipate Objections**
- List top 10 investor concerns (market size, competition, team, traction, exit, etc.)
- Prepare 30-second responses for each
- Practice with friend playing skeptical investor

**3. Rehearse Timing**
- Full deck: 10-12 minutes (leave 15-20 min for Q&A in 30-min meeting)
- Elevator pitch: 3 minutes (problem, solution, traction, ask)
- One-liner: 15 seconds ("IonWave is marine plasma electrolytes — LMNT's value prop + Seaonic's quality")

**4. Test Technology**
- Zoom screen share: Make sure deck is readable (text size, resolution)
- Video on/off: Present with video on (builds trust, shows confidence)
- Backup plan: Have PDF version (if Google Slides fails, send PDF in chat)

### During Presentation (Confidence: A)

**1. Start Strong**
- First 30 seconds: Hook investor ("We're solving X problem for Y audience, and we're raising $Z")
- Don't start with "Hi, my name is..." (they know who you are) — start with the problem

**2. Tell a Story**
- Narrative arc: Problem (pain) → Solution (relief) → Traction (proof) → Ask (next chapter)
- Personal anecdote: "I was training for a marathon and couldn't find clean electrolytes..." (if authentic)

**3. Pause for Questions**
- Don't rush through deck monologue-style
- After key slides (problem, solution, financials), pause: "Any questions so far?"
- If investor asks question, answer fully (don't say "I'll get to that later" — they may forget)

**4. Read the Room**
- Engaged investor: Leaning forward, asking questions, taking notes → slow down, let them drive
- Distracted investor: Checking phone, multitasking, short responses → speed up or wrap early (not a fit)
- Skeptical investor: Arms crossed, frowning, challenging → address concerns directly ("I sense you're concerned about X — let me address that")

**5. Handle Objections Gracefully**
- Acknowledge: "That's a great question" (not "that's wrong")
- Answer: Provide data, logic, or evidence
- Validate: "Does that address your concern, or should I dig deeper?"
- Don't get defensive: Investor skepticism is due diligence, not personal attack

**6. Close with Clear Next Steps**
- End with ask: "We're raising $30K on a SAFE at $250K cap — would you be interested in participating?"
- If yes: "Great, I'll send over the SAFE docs and we can schedule a follow-up call"
- If maybe: "What additional information would help you make a decision?"
- If no: "No problem — can you share what gave you pause? Any intros to other investors who might be a better fit?"

### Post-Presentation Follow-Up (Confidence: A)

**1. Send Thank-You Email (Within 24 Hours)**

> Subject: Thank you — IonWave deck + next steps
>
> Hi [Investor Name],
>
> Thanks for taking the time to meet today. As discussed, I'm attaching the deck and our SAFE docs ($30K at $250K cap).
>
> Next steps:
> - I'll send Week 4 traction update (first sales data) by [date]
> - Happy to schedule follow-up call to answer any additional questions
> - Let me know if you'd like intro to [advisor/customer/partner] for reference
>
> Best,
> Danilo

**2. Weekly Updates (If Interested)**
- Share progress: "Week 4 update: Sold 12 units at $38 CAC, 1 repeat customer — ahead of plan"
- Keep it short: 3-4 bullets, no essay
- Momentum matters: Consistent progress updates increase investor confidence

**3. Ask for Feedback (If Pass)**
- "What would need to be true for you to invest?" (learn for next pitch)
- "Any intros to other investors who might be a better fit?" (expand network)
- Stay in touch: Add to quarterly update list (they may invest in future round)

## Alternative Formats

### 1. One-Pager (Confidence: B)

**When to Use:**
- Cold email attachment (lighter than full deck)
- Investor intro requests (easy for connector to forward)
- Conference/event handouts (quick reference)

**Content:**
- Top half: Logo, tagline, one-sentence problem, one-sentence solution
- Middle: Key metrics (revenue, units sold, CAC, LTV, margin) or traction plan (if pre-revenue)
- Bottom: Team (founder photo + 1-sentence bio), Ask ($30K SAFE at $250K cap), Contact

**IonWave One-Pager Example:**

```
[Logo] IonWave — Premium Marine Plasma Electrolytes

Problem: Existing electrolytes are incomplete (5-7 minerals) or low-quality (sugar, artificial).
Solution: Marine plasma with 78 minerals — LMNT's value prop + Seaonic's quality.

Traction Plan (Pre-Revenue):
- Week 3: Launch ads (target CTR ≥1.5%)
- Week 4: First sales (target ≥5 units, CAC ≤$50)
- Week 7: Raise/kill decision (target ≥20 units, CAC ≤$40)

Business Model:
- Pricing: $47-59 per 24-sachet box
- Margin: 65-75%
- LTV:CAC target: 5-6x

Team: Danilo [Last Name], Founder — [1-sentence bio]

Ask: $30-50K SAFE at $250-500K cap (use: 33% inventory, 30% ads, 37% working capital)

Contact: [email] | [LinkedIn] | [website]
```

**Tools:** Canva (templates), Google Docs (text-heavy), Figma (design-heavy)

### 2. Loom Video Pitch (Confidence: C)

**When to Use:**
- Supplement to deck (investor requested more detail)
- Cold outreach differentiation (video stands out vs text email)
- Async diligence (investor can watch on their timeline)

**Structure:**
- Length: 3-5 minutes (max 8 minutes)
- Format: Screen share (deck) + webcam (face in corner)
- Content: Walk through key slides (problem, solution, traction, ask), emphasize 2-3 most important points

**Script Example:**

> "Hi, I'm Danilo, founder of IonWave. I'm raising $30K to validate a marine plasma electrolyte brand.
>
> [Slide 2] The problem: Existing electrolytes are incomplete — LMNT has only 5 minerals, Seaonic is $3/serving. Athletes and biohackers want complete hydration without compromise.
>
> [Slide 3] Our solution: Marine plasma with 78 minerals — ocean-isotonic, bioavailable, clean label. We're positioned between LMNT and Seaonic.
>
> [Slide 6] We're running a 7-week validation sprint: Week 3 ads, Week 4 first sales, Week 7 raise/kill decision. Clear success criteria: ≥20 units, CAC ≤$40.
>
> [Slide 12] We're raising $30K on a SAFE at $250K cap. Use: 33% inventory, 30% ads, 37% working capital.
>
> If this resonates, I'd love to schedule a call to discuss. Thanks for watching!"

**Pros:**
- Personal connection (investor sees/hears founder)
- Shows confidence and communication skills
- Differentiation (most founders send decks, not videos)
