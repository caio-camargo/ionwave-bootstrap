## Part 1: Structure/Function vs. Disease Claims (50+ Examples)

### 1.1 The Bright Line Rule

**Structure/Function Claims** (ALLOWED — notify FDA within 30 days):
- Describe the role of a nutrient/ingredient in affecting normal structure/function
- Characterize documented mechanism by which nutrient acts to maintain structure/function
- Describe general well-being from consumption

**Disease Claims** (PROHIBITED without drug approval):
- Claim to diagnose, mitigate, treat, cure, or prevent a disease
- Reference a specific disease or class of diseases
- Use disease-associated terms (high blood pressure, diabetes, inflammation, etc.)
- Imply product treats abnormal conditions caused by disease

[Confidence: A | Source: FDA 21 CFR 101.93(f), FDA Small Entity Compliance Guide on Structure/Function Claims]

### 1.3 Mechanism Claims (Lower Risk)

**Mechanistic claims** describe HOW something works without claiming a health outcome.

**Examples of SAFE mechanism claims**:
- ✅ "Ionic minerals are water-soluble"
- ✅ "Minerals support cellular energy production" (describes mechanism, not outcome)
- ✅ "Electrolytes help regulate fluid balance" (describes physiological role)
- ✅ "Magnesium is required for over 300 enzymatic reactions"

**Why these are safer**:
- Describe established biochemistry, not health outcomes
- Don't claim to treat/prevent disease
- Can be substantiated with peer-reviewed literature (no RCT needed)

[Confidence: A | Source: FDA Guidance on Nutrient Content Claims]

## Part 2: Claims Substantiation Hierarchy

### 2.1 FTC "Reasonable Basis" Standard

**The FTC requires substantiation BEFORE making a claim.** The level of substantiation depends on claim type:

| Claim Type | Substantiation Required | Evidence Grade | Example |
|------------|-------------------------|----------------|---------|
| **Absolute Performance Claim** | Randomized Controlled Trial (RCT), published | A-grade | "Increases energy by 30% in 2 weeks" |
| **Comparative Claim** | Head-to-head study OR strong indirect evidence | A or B | "Better absorbed than synthetic minerals" |
| **Testimonial (typical results)** | RCT showing typical outcomes | A-grade | "Most users report more energy within 2 weeks" |
| **Testimonial (atypical results)** | Real customer + "results not typical" disclaimer | B-grade | "I felt amazing — results may vary" |
| **General Benefit** | Competent and reliable scientific evidence | B-grade | "Supports hydration and energy" |
| **Mechanism (no performance claim)** | Peer-reviewed studies on mechanism | B-grade | "Ionic minerals support cellular function" |
| **Factual Statement** | Verifiable fact | A-grade | "Contains 78 minerals" / "Ocean-sourced" |

[Confidence: A | Source: FTC Policy Statement on Deception, FTC v. POM Wonderful (2016), FTC Health Products Compliance Guide]

---

#### CATEGORY 3: General Health Benefits (B-C Grade, Moderate-High Risk)

**These require "competent and reliable scientific evidence" — peer-reviewed studies showing benefit:**

| Claim | Substantiation | IonWave Status |
|-------|----------------|----------------|
| "Supports hydration" | Studies on electrolyte role in hydration | ✅ LIKELY COMPLIANT (well-established science) |
| "Supports energy production" | Studies on mineral deficiency → fatigue | ✅ LIKELY COMPLIANT (well-established) |
| "Supports recovery after exercise" | Studies on electrolyte replenishment | ✅ LIKELY COMPLIANT (well-established) |
| "Supports mental clarity" | Studies linking mineral status to cognition | ⚠️ WEAKER (correlation studies exist, but not RCTs on marine plasma) |

**Risk Assessment**: General health benefits framed as "supports [function]" are low-risk IF:
- The mechanism is well-established in peer-reviewed literature
- You're not claiming quantified outcomes (e.g., "30% more energy")
- You're not claiming to treat disease

---

#### CATEGORY 4: Performance/Quantified Claims (A-Grade Required, HIGH RISK)

**These REQUIRE RCT on the specific product (not just the ingredient category):**

| Claim | Substantiation | IonWave Status |
|-------|----------------|----------------|
| "98% bioavailable" | RCT comparing marine plasma to synthetic minerals | ❌ NOT SUBSTANTIATED — REMOVE OR SOFTEN |
| "Increases HRV by 15 points" | RCT measuring HRV pre/post | ❌ NOT SUBSTANTIATED — FICTIONAL CLAIM |
| "Improves energy by 30%" | RCT with energy self-reports | ❌ NOT SUBSTANTIATED — REMOVE |
| "Better than LMNT" (performance) | Head-to-head RCT | ❌ NOT SUBSTANTIATED — REMOVE OR SOFTEN TO FACTUAL |

**FTC Penalty Range for Unsubstantiated Performance Claims**: $100K-$5M+ depending on harm.

---

### 2.3 Upgrade Path: When to Commission an RCT

**Commission an RCT if:**
- Revenue > $1M/year (can afford $50K-$200K study cost)
- Performance claims are core to differentiation (e.g., "98% bioavailable" is THE story)
- Scaling to 7-figures requires aggressive claims to compete

**RCT Scope for IonWave** (if pursuing):
- **Primary endpoint**: Serum mineral levels (magnesium, potassium, sodium) pre/post supplementation
- **Secondary endpoints**: Self-reported energy, hydration markers, HRV
- **Comparator**: Synthetic electrolyte blend (e.g., LMNT-style formula)
- **Sample size**: 60-100 participants (powered for statistical significance)
- **Duration**: 4-8 weeks
- **Cost**: $50K-$200K (academic partnership may reduce cost)
- **Timeline**: 6-12 months (IRB approval + recruitment + data collection + publication)

**Alternative (Cheaper)**: Commission observational study ($10K-$30K) to collect user-reported outcomes, but this supports testimonials only (not absolute claims).

[Confidence: B | Source: Clinical trial cost benchmarks, FTC substantiation case law]

#### VIOLATION #2: Unsubstantiated Bioavailability Claim

**Location**: M11 VSL, Solution Section [1:30-2:00]

**Claim**: "He found that human blood plasma is 98% identical to seawater."

**Risk Level**: ❌ **HIGH — FTC SUBSTANTIATION RISK**

**Issue**:
- Quinton's 1897 finding pre-dates modern chemistry
- "98% identical" oversimplifies (refers to mineral composition, not chemical structure)
- Modern use of this claim requires RCT substantiation OR heavy context

**FTC Precedent**: FTC v. POM Wonderful (2016) — "clinically proven" claims require RCT, even if based on historical use

**Correction Options**:
1. **Soften** (compliant): "René Quinton observed similarities between blood plasma and seawater mineral composition"
2. **Context** (moderate risk): "René Quinton found that seawater contains minerals in ratios similar to blood plasma — a finding that led to medical use of marine plasma in early 1900s France"
3. **Remove** (safest): Cut the "98% identical" language entirely

**Recommended**: Option #1 (soften) — preserves story without FTC risk

### 4.1 Monitoring Competitors for FTC Violations

- Understand competitive positioning within compliance boundaries

- **LMNT competitors** (smaller DTC brands)

---

### 4.2 Competitor Audit Checklist

**For each competitor, review their website, ads, and social media for:**

#### CATEGORY 1: Testimonial Compliance
- [ ] Are testimonials real customers (not actors/stock photos)?
- [ ] Do testimonials include "results not typical" disclaimer?
- [ ] Are influencer posts properly disclosed (#ad, "paid partnership")?
- [ ] Are there quantified claims in testimonials (e.g., "lost 10 lbs") without typical results disclaimer?

**Red Flags**: Fake testimonials, undisclosed influencer posts, quantified claims without disclaimers

---

#### CATEGORY 2: Performance Claims
- [ ] Are there quantified health claims (e.g., "30% more energy")?
- [ ] Do they cite clinical studies to support claims?
- [ ] Are there comparative superiority claims (e.g., "better than X")?
- [ ] Do they use terms like "clinically proven" or "scientifically validated"?

**Red Flags**: Unsubstantiated performance claims, fake studies, misleading comparisons

---

#### CATEGORY 3: Disease Claims
- [ ] Do they use disease terms (e.g., "treats dehydration," "prevents illness")?
- [ ] Do they reference medical conditions (e.g., "for people with high blood pressure")?
- [ ] Do they use terms like "cure," "treat," "prevent," "diagnose"?

**Red Flags**: Any disease claim without drug approval (FDA will send warning letter)

---

#### CATEGORY 4: Ingredient Claims
- [ ] Do they claim specific ingredient efficacy (e.g., "proven to boost immunity")?
- [ ] Do ingredient claims match peer-reviewed literature?
- [ ] Do they cite sources for ingredient claims?

**Red Flags**: Exaggerated ingredient claims, cherry-picked studies, fake science
