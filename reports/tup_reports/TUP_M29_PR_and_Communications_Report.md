## Purpose

Define IonWave's crisis response protocols. Danilo's crisis playbook was thin (5 crisis types, one-line responses) and the viral PR tab was better but still generic. This file merges both into a supplement-brand-specific crisis playbook that accounts for the unique risks of selling health products.

**Why this matters for a supplement brand**: Health supplements operate in a regulatory minefield. A single unsubstantiated health claim on social media can trigger an FTC investigation. A contamination scare (even a false one) can destroy a brand overnight. A competitor or troll posting fake reviews can tank conversion rates. Having a pre-written playbook means you respond correctly under pressure, not emotionally.

**The #1 rule of crisis communications**: Speed beats perfection. A fast, empathetic acknowledgment within 2 hours beats a perfect corporate statement released 48 hours later.

---

## 1. Crisis Severity Framework

### 1.1 Severity Levels

| Level | Definition | Response Speed | Who Decides | Example |
|-------|-----------|----------------|-------------|---------|
| **1 — Low** | Single negative comment or review. Contained, not spreading. | Within 24 hrs | Founder (or support) | One bad review on Shopify or Amazon |
| **2 — Medium** | Influencer criticism or multiple complaints about the same issue. Moderate visibility. | Within 4-6 hrs | Founder | Influencer posts criticism to 10K+ followers |
| **3 — High** | Viral complaint or social media pile-on. Spreading rapidly. 100+ comments/shares. | Within 1-2 hrs | Founder + advisor if available | Customer post about bad experience goes viral on Reddit/TikTok |
| **4 — Critical** | Media coverage of a negative story, regulatory action, or product safety issue. | Immediately | Founder + legal counsel | FDA warning letter, contamination report, news article |

### 1.2 Escalation Triggers

Move to the NEXT severity level when:
- A Level 1 issue gets picked up by an influencer → Level 2
- A Level 2 issue gets shared 50+ times or media contacts you → Level 3
- A Level 3 issue involves product safety or regulatory bodies → Level 4
- Any issue involves: physical harm, legal action, regulatory action → Immediately Level 4

## 2. Crisis Response Protocols

### 2.1 General Response Framework (All Levels)

The 6-A Framework (from Danilo's tab 622, expanded):

```
1. ASSESS — Is this real? How serious? How fast is it spreading?
   - Verify the claim. Is the complaint legitimate or manufactured?
   - Check: How many people are seeing this? Is it growing?
   - Screenshot everything (comments, posts, DMs) for records.

2. ACKNOWLEDGE — Respond quickly. Show you're listening.
   - "We've seen this and we're looking into it."
   - Do NOT wait for a full investigation before acknowledging.
   - Acknowledge publicly where the complaint was made (don't hide in DMs).

3. APOLOGIZE — If warranted, take responsibility.
   - "We're sorry this happened. This isn't acceptable."
   - Only apologize for things that are actually your fault.
   - Don't apologize for false accusations (but don't be defensive either).

4. ACT — Explain what you're doing to fix it.
   - "Here's what we're doing right now..."
   - Concrete actions, not vague promises.
   - Offer a remedy (refund, replacement, discount, etc.)

5. UPDATE — Keep people informed until resolved.
   - "Update: We've completed X, Y, Z..."
   - Post follow-up even if the original post has died down.
   - People remember brands that follow through.

6. ADAPT — What do you change to prevent this?
   - Post-crisis internal review
   - Process change, quality improvement, policy update
   - Document the lesson learned
```

### 2.2 Do's and Don'ts

**DO:**
- Respond quickly (silence = guilt in the public's mind)
- Be human, not corporate (write like a person, not a PR firm)
- Take responsibility where appropriate
- Move conversation to private channels for resolution details
- Follow up publicly after the private resolution ("This has been resolved — [customer] has been taken care of")
- Document everything (screenshots, timestamps, responses)

**DON'T:**
- Delete negative comments (screenshots exist; this makes it 10x worse)
- Get defensive or argumentative (never argue with a customer publicly)
- Ignore it hoping it goes away (it won't — it festers)
- Make excuses (customers don't care about your supply chain problems)
- Lie or mislead (one dishonest response can end a brand)
- Respond emotionally (draft a response, wait 15 minutes, then review before posting)
- Use legal threats against customers publicly (use legal channels privately if needed)

## 3. Supplement-Specific Crisis Scenarios

### 3.1 Product Quality / Adverse Reaction

**Scenario**: A customer reports feeling sick after using IonWave. Posts on social media.

**Severity**: Level 3-4 depending on specifics

**Response protocol**:
1. **Immediate** (within 1 hour): "We take this very seriously. We're reaching out to you directly right now. Our products undergo [quality testing process]. We want to understand exactly what happened."
2. **Private DM**: Contact customer directly. Get details: batch number, symptoms, timeline, other products/medications, medical history (if they'll share).
3. **Internal**: Check batch COA (Certificate of Analysis). Check for other complaints from same batch. Contact supplier if needed.
4. **If product issue confirmed**: Stop sales of affected batch. Notify all customers who purchased from that batch. Offer full refund + replacement from different batch. Post public update about the issue and resolution.
5. **If product is NOT the cause**: Follow up with customer privately, offer refund regardless (goodwill), post general response about quality standards without dismissing the customer's experience.
6. **Regulatory**: If the adverse event is serious, submit an Adverse Event Report to FDA (MedWatch). This is legally required for serious adverse events.

**Pre-built response template**:
> "We take your health seriously — that's why we exist. We're looking into this right now and reaching out to you directly. Our products are third-party tested with a Certificate of Analysis for every batch. We want to understand exactly what happened. Please check your DMs."

[Confidence: B | Source: FDA adverse event reporting requirements (21 CFR 190.6), supplement industry crisis response best practices.]

### 3.2 FTC/FDA Regulatory Issue

**Scenario**: FTC sends a warning letter about a health claim on your website or social media. Or FDA flags a labeling issue.

**Severity**: Level 4 — always

**Response protocol**:
1. **Do NOT respond publicly** before consulting legal counsel.
2. **Internal**: Immediately review the specific claim or label cited. Identify every instance (website, social, ads, packaging).
3. **Legal**: Contact attorney who specializes in FTC/FDA supplement compliance. Cost: $200-500/hour, but non-negotiable.
4. **Remediate**: Remove or modify the cited content within the timeline specified (usually 15-30 days for warning letters).
5. **Respond to agency**: Formal written response per legal counsel's guidance.
6. **If it becomes public**: "We received feedback from [agency] about [specific issue] and have already made the requested changes. Product safety and regulatory compliance are non-negotiable for us."

**Prevention** (most important):
- Never use "cure," "treat," "prevent," or "diagnose" language for supplements
- Include FDA disclaimer on all supplement content: "These statements have not been evaluated by the Food and Drug Administration. This product is not intended to diagnose, treat, cure, or prevent any disease."
- Review all ad copy and social media for implied health claims
- See M7 (Regulatory) for comprehensive compliance checklist

[Confidence: B | Source: FTC enforcement actions against supplement brands are public record. Warning letter process is well-documented.]

### 3.3 Negative Viral Post / Influencer Attack

**Scenario**: A TikTok creator with 500K+ followers posts a video criticizing IonWave — calling it "overpriced" or "just salt water."

**Severity**: Level 3

**Response protocol**:
1. **Assess** (30 minutes): Watch the full video. Is the criticism factual or unfair? Note specific claims.
2. **Don't engage in the comments** of their video (you'll be dunked on).
3. **Post a response on your OWN channel** (if you have enough following to be heard):
   - Acknowledge the criticism gracefully: "We saw [creator]'s video and appreciate the conversation"
   - Address specific factual claims with evidence (lab test results, COA, ingredient comparison)
   - Be confident but not arrogant
   - Invite the creator to try the product or have a conversation
4. **If the criticism is fair**: Own it. "They made a fair point about [X]. We're working on it." Authenticity wins.
5. **Leverage your community**: Don't ask customers to attack the creator. But if loyal customers defend the brand organically, amplify their responses.
6. **Wait 48-72 hours**: Most viral negativity dies in 2-3 days. If it's still growing, escalate.

**What NOT to do**: Send a cease-and-desist to a TikTok creator. This backfires spectacularly 100% of the time.

[Confidence: B | Source: Multiple DTC brand crisis case studies. The "don't fight in their comments" rule is hard-won industry wisdom.]

### 3.4 Fake Reviews / Competitor Sabotage

**Scenario**: A batch of clearly fake 1-star reviews appears on your Shopify store or Amazon listing.

**Severity**: Level 2

**Response protocol**:
1. **Document**: Screenshot every suspected fake review with timestamps.
2. **Flag**: Report to platform (Shopify, Amazon, Trustpilot) using their fake review reporting tools.
3. **Respond publicly**: Reply to each review professionally: "We have no record of this order. If you're a genuine customer, please contact us at [email] so we can make it right."
4. **Activate real customers**: Send an email to your best customers asking for honest reviews (do NOT incentivize — this violates FTC guidelines).
5. **If Amazon**: File a Brand Registry report if you have trademark protection.

[Confidence: B | Source: Amazon seller community — fake review attacks are common. Response playbook is well-documented.]

### 3.5 Ad Account Ban

**Scenario**: Meta (Facebook/Instagram) bans your ad account — common for supplement brands due to health claim policies.

**Severity**: Level 2-3 (business impact is immediate — paid acquisition stops)

**Response protocol**:
1. **Appeal immediately** through Meta Business Help Center. Be specific about which ads you believe comply with policy.
2. **Review all active ads**: Identify any that could violate health claim policies. Fix them before the appeal.
3. **Diversify immediately**: Shift budget to Google Ads, TikTok Ads, or other channels temporarily.
4. **Backup account**: Best practice is to have a backup ad account ready (different Business Manager, same business entity) — set this up BEFORE you need it.
5. **Preventive**: Submit ads for manual review before scaling. Use "structure/function" claim language only. See M13 (Media Buying) for ad compliance guidelines.

**Common triggers for supplement ad account bans**:
- Before/after images (even if real and truthful)
- "Cures," "treats," "prevents" language
- Unrealistic health promises
- Targeting health conditions directly
- User-generated content with health claims that you amplify

[Confidence: B | Source: DTC supplement advertiser community. Meta ad account bans are well-documented; supplement brands are disproportionately affected.]

### 3.6 Supply Chain Failure / Stockout

**Scenario**: Supplier can't deliver. Product is out of stock for 2+ weeks.

**Severity**: Level 2 (Level 3 if subscribers are affected)

**Response protocol**:
1. **Communicate immediately**: Email all subscribers explaining the situation and expected timeline.
2. **Pause charges**: Do NOT charge subscribers for product you can't ship. Pause subscription billing proactively.
3. **Offer alternatives**: "We're extending your next subscription by [X] weeks at no cost" or "Here's a 20% discount on your next order."
4. **Social media**: Post transparent update. Customers respect honesty about supply issues.
5. **Prevent**: Maintain 2-3 months of safety stock. Identify backup supplier before you need one.

[Confidence: B | Source: DTC supply chain management best practices. Transparent communication during stockouts is well-documented as the right approach.]

### 3.7 Chargeback Spike

**Scenario**: Chargeback rate exceeds 1% — payment processor threatens account suspension.

**Severity**: Level 3 (existential threat — losing payment processing kills the business)

**Response protocol**:
1. **Investigate immediately**: Are chargebacks from legitimate customers or fraud?
2. **Customer service**: Reach out to every chargeback customer before they escalate.
3. **Fraud**: Implement Address Verification (AVS) and 3D Secure if not already active.
4. **Subscription clarity**: Ensure subscription terms are crystal clear at checkout. Most supplement chargebacks come from "I didn't know I was subscribing."
5. **Preventive**: Easy cancellation process (hard cancellation = chargebacks). Clear email reminders before each subscription charge. See M21 (Subscription) for retention vs. forced retention.

[Confidence: B | Source: Payment processing industry standards. 1% chargeback threshold is standard across Stripe/Shopify Payments.]

### 3.8 Competitive Claim Copying *(Dialogue upgrade U3)*

**Scenario**: A competitor starts using "78 minerals," "marine plasma," or "ocean-sourced electrolyte" messaging — copying IonWave's core positioning.

**Severity**: Level 2 (brand threat, not immediate crisis)

**Response protocol**:
1. **Document**: Screenshot competitor's claims with dates. Establish your prior usage timeline.
2. **Legal assessment** (private): Consult M7 (Regulatory) — do you have trademark protection for "marine plasma" or specific formulation claims? If yes, send a cease-and-desist through legal counsel. If no, consider filing trademarks now.
3. **Double down on content** (public): Publish more marine plasma science content (M16). The brand with the deepest content library owns the narrative. Your 50+ articles on ocean minerals are a moat no copycat can match overnight.
4. **Lean into sourcing story**: Competitors can copy the language, but they can't copy your specific supplier relationship, COA documentation, or founder story. Make the sourcing story more prominent.
5. **DO NOT**: Publicly call out the competitor. Don't post "they copied us." It looks petty and draws attention to them. Win by being deeper, not louder.
6. **Long game**: The first brand to build topical authority in a niche usually holds it. Content moat + brand recognition + customer loyalty = defensible positioning.

[Confidence: C | Source: Brand competitive strategy. Trademark law is jurisdiction-specific — consult attorney for specifics.]

## 4. Monitoring & Early Detection

### 4.1 Monitoring Setup (Pre-Launch)

| Tool | Cost | What It Monitors | Setup Time |
|------|------|-----------------|------------|
| **Google Alerts** | Free | Brand name, product name, competitor mentions | 10 min |
| **Mention** | $29/month (when revenue supports) | Social media, news, forums, blogs | 30 min |
| **Shopify review notifications** | Free (built-in) | New product reviews | Automatic |
| **Social media notifications** | Free | @mentions, DMs, comments | 5 min |
| **Google Search Console** | Free | Brand name search queries (spike in "[brand] scam" = early warning) | Already set up (M16) |

### 4.2 Daily Monitoring Routine (5 minutes)

1. Check Google Alerts email digest
2. Scan social media notifications/DMs
3. Check review platforms for new reviews
4. Quick Google search: "[brand name]" — look for anything unexpected on first page

### 4.3 Early Warning Signals

| Signal | What It Means | Action |
|--------|-------------|--------|
| Spike in "[brand] scam" searches (GSC) | Someone is questioning legitimacy | Investigate source, proactively address |
| Cluster of similar complaints (email/DMs) | Possible product or shipping issue | Investigate batch quality, shipping partner |
| Negative post gaining traction (50+ engagements) | Early viral potential | Engage with 6-A framework before it escalates |
| Sudden drop in ad performance | Possible account flagging or negative sentiment | Check ad account status, review recent publicity |
| Competitor attacking by name | Competitive PR tactic | Don't engage directly. Win by being better, not by fighting. |

## 5. Pre-Crisis Preparedness Checklist

Set these up BEFORE a crisis happens:

- [ ] **Press kit ready**: One-page PDF with brand story, founder bio, product photos, key statistics. Updated quarterly.
- [ ] **Legal counsel identified**: Attorney who specializes in FTC/FDA supplement law. Know their name, email, and hourly rate before you need them.
- [ ] **Response templates saved**: Pre-written drafts for each crisis scenario (sections 3.1-3.7 above). Customize per situation, don't use verbatim.
- [ ] **Monitoring active**: Google Alerts, social notifications, review alerts all configured.
- [ ] **Backup ad account**: Second Business Manager set up on Meta. Not activated, but ready.
- [ ] **Customer communication channel**: Email list segmented so you can quickly reach all subscribers, all one-time buyers, or all recent customers.
- [ ] **COA documentation**: Certificate of Analysis for every batch easily accessible. Can be produced within 1 hour of any product quality question.
- [ ] **FDA disclaimer**: On every product page, blog post, social media bio, and ad. No exceptions.

## 2. Podcast Concept (For When It Launches)

### 2.1 Concept Design

| Element | Decision | Rationale |
|---------|----------|-----------|
| **Working Title** | "Mineral Intelligence" or "The Hydration Lab" | Science-forward, not brand-forward. The podcast should stand on its own, not be a 30-min ad. |
| **Tagline** | "The science of what your body actually needs" | Broad enough to attract wellness audience, specific enough to differentiate |
| **Format** | Mix: 60% interview + 40% solo deep-dive | Interviews bring guests (relationship building + cross-promotion). Solo episodes showcase founder expertise. |
| **Length** | 20-30 minutes | Short enough that listeners finish episodes (completion rate matters for Apple/Spotify algorithms). Health/wellness listeners prefer concise. |
| **Frequency** | Bi-weekly (every 2 weeks) | Realistic for a founder. Weekly burns out fast. Bi-weekly is sustainable and still builds momentum. |
| **Target Listener** | Health-curious adults (30-50) interested in evidence-based wellness | Broader than "electrolyte buyers" — the podcast should attract people who might become customers, not just existing ones. |
| **Differentiator** | Founder-scientist voice — not a journalist or influencer, but someone who actually formulates the product and reads the studies | Authenticity is the competitive advantage. Most wellness podcasts are hosted by non-experts. |

### 2.2 Episode Framework

**Interview episodes (60%)**:
- 5 min: Host intro + guest introduction
- 15-20 min: Conversation (guided by 4-5 prepared questions, but conversational)
- 3-5 min: Rapid-fire Q&A or audience questions
- 1-2 min: Guest plugs + host outro with CTA

**Solo episodes (40%)**:
- 2 min: Hook + what you'll learn
- 15-20 min: Deep-dive on topic (science, data, practical advice)
- 3-5 min: Summary + CTA

**CTA discipline**: One soft CTA per episode. "If you're interested in ocean mineral electrolytes, check out IonWave at [website]." NOT a 5-minute sales pitch. The podcast builds trust; the website closes sales.

## 1. Why Video (and Why Not Yet)

### 1.1 The Case FOR Video

- YouTube is the 2nd largest search engine — video content ranks in Google
- Video builds trust faster than text (face, voice, personality)
- Evergreen video content compounds views over time
- One video generates multiple assets: clips, social posts, thumbnails, transcripts → blog posts
- Supplement/health content performs well on YouTube (large audience demand)

### 1.2 The Case for DEFERRING Video

- Video production takes 3-5x more time per piece than blog content
- YouTube algorithm rewards consistency — sporadic posting is worse than not posting
- Blog/SEO content has better per-hour ROI for a solo founder in Year 1
- Video equipment, editing, and optimization have a learning curve
- A bad video (poor audio, lighting, pacing) can actively hurt brand perception

### 1.3 The Verdict

**Defer YouTube as a channel until Month 4-6.** Focus Phase 0-1 entirely on blog content (M16) and paid ads (M13). Video enters the mix only when:
1. Blog content cadence is established (1-2 posts/week running smoothly)
2. Paid ads have a winning creative (Milestone 3: Winner Found)
3. Either: Caio allocates dedicated video time, OR a part-time editor is hired ($200-500/month freelance)

**Exception**: Short-form video (TikTok/Reels, 30-60 seconds) can start earlier because it's low production. Talking-head clips filmed on iPhone, no editing needed. This is covered in M16's social media distribution strategy.

## 4. Shorts Strategy

YouTube Shorts (and TikTok/Reels) deserve a separate strategy because they operate on different algorithms:

### 4.1 Shorts Content Types

| Type | Example | Goal | Effort |

| **Science nuggets** | "Did you know ocean water has 78 minerals?" | Education | Low |

### 4.2 Shorts Best Practices

- **Hook in first 1-2 seconds** — no intro, no logo, jump straight to the interesting thing
- **Vertical format** (9:16), 15-60 seconds
- **Text overlay** — assume no sound (many viewers watch muted)
- **Post consistently** — 3-5 Shorts/week is manageable; daily is ideal if batch-filming
- **Batch-film**: Set up once, film 10-15 Shorts in one session (1-2 hours → 2-3 weeks of content)
